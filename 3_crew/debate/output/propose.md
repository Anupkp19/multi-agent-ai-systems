There must be strict laws to regulate LLMs for three core reasons: power, opacity, and irreversibility.

**1. LLMs already wield enormous power over information and choice.**  
LLMs are rapidly becoming gatekeepers of knowledge, work, and even political discourse. They will draft legislation, filter news, guide medical questions, shape children’s learning, and mediate how billions of people understand the world. Any system that can subtly steer what people see, think, and decide is not a toy—it is critical infrastructure. We regulate other powerful infrastructures—air travel, pharmaceuticals, financial markets—not because we hate innovation, but because the cost of failure is catastrophic. Leaving LLMs largely to self-regulation is equivalent to letting drug companies approve their own medicines or banks set their own capital rules. That is not prudence; it is negligence.

**2. The technology is fundamentally opaque and unaccountable without law.**  
LLMs are not like a book, whose contents you can fully inspect, nor like a traditional program with explicit, auditable rules. They are vast black-box systems trained on unknown data, encoding unknown biases, and producing outputs that are probabilistic and often wrong, but extremely persuasive. When such a system:

- Fabricates medical advice that harms someone,  
- Generates defamatory falsehoods about a private individual,  
- Enables targeted scams or deepfake political content,

who is responsible? The developer? The deployer? The user? Without strict legal standards—on due diligence, safety testing, record-keeping, and liability—everyone can point fingers at everyone else, and the victim is left with no recourse. Law is the only mechanism that can turn a diffuse “shared responsibility” slogan into concrete accountability.

**3. The externalities are massive and unpriced.**  
LLMs can cause large-scale harms that no single actor has an incentive to prevent:

- **Misinformation and manipulation:** Cheap, tailored propaganda and deepfakes can distort elections, inflame ethnic tensions, or fabricate “evidence” in court of public opinion.  
- **Cybercrime and weapons assistance:** Systems that trivially help with malware, fraud scripts, or weapon design even at a “slightly reduced” level still meaningfully lower the barrier to serious harm.  
- **Labor disruption:** The speed and scale at which LLMs can displace white‑collar work could outpace any natural adjustment, harming millions without any systematic transition planning.  
- **Privacy and data abuse:** Training on scraped personal data, proprietary documents, or sensitive communications can expose information at scale and permanently.

In economic terms, these are negative externalities: costs pushed onto society that creators do not pay. Strict regulation is how we force those who build and deploy LLMs to internalize the risks they create—through safety requirements, audits, and liability—so they design systems with society’s welfare in mind, not just speed and profit.

**4. “Self-regulation” has already failed in analogous domains.**  
We’ve seen this movie before:

- Social media platforms promised to self-regulate misinformation and extremism; we got algorithmic amplification of outrage, foreign interference in elections, and years of reactive, opaque content moderation.  
- Financial institutions promised to self-regulate complex financial products; we got the 2008 crisis.  
- Tech companies promised to self-police privacy; we got mass, opaque data harvesting and recurring breaches.

LLMs are even more general-purpose and harder for laypeople to understand. Expecting voluntary codes, PR statements, and “AI ethics boards” funded by the very companies at stake to adequately protect the public is wishful thinking. Without enforceable rules, “ethics” becomes a branding exercise, not a constraint.

**5. Strict does not mean simplistic; it means enforceable, targeted, and binding.**  
“Strict laws” are not about banning innovation. They are about setting hard floors below which no system is allowed to fall. For example:

- **Mandatory safety and robustness testing** before deployment of powerful models, just as we require clinical trials for drugs.  
- **Independent audits and transparency obligations** about training data sources, high‑risk use cases, and system limitations.  
- **Clear liability frameworks** so that if an LLM system foreseeably enables serious harm and the provider ignored known risks, they are legally accountable.  
- **Red lines** on especially dangerous applications (e.g., autonomous cyber‑offense, automated generation of targeted disinformation at scale, direct weapons design).

These are “strict” in the sense that they are not optional and have real penalties, but they can be carefully scoped to high‑risk systems and uses. That preserves space for low‑risk innovation while putting guardrails where the stakes are highest.

**6. Irreversibility demands caution before capability, not after.**  
Once highly capable LLMs are widely deployed:

- Harmful behaviors and misuse pathways will have been discovered by bad actors and diffused globally.  
- Economic structures and dependencies will have formed around them, making rollback politically and practically impossible.  
- Models and weights will be copied, leaked, and open-sourced; you cannot “un-release” capability.

This is an asymmetry: if we regulate too loosely and get it wrong, we may not get a second chance. If we regulate strictly and discover we were overly cautious, laws can be relaxed. History shows it is far easier to loosen regulation than to re‑contain a technology that has already caused systemic damage. With irreversible technologies, prudence is not optional; it is an ethical duty.

**7. Democratic control over a general-purpose cognitive technology is non‑negotiable.**  
LLMs are not just another app. They are a general method for cheaply generating language, code, images, and decisions that approximate human reasoning across almost any domain. Concentrating that capability in the hands of a few unregulated corporations gives those companies de facto power over:

- What information is emphasized or downplayed  
- Which languages and cultures are preserved or neglected  
- Which ethical and political norms are “baked into” default answers

Deciding how such systems behave is a societal choice, not a private product design decision. The only legitimate way to make that choice is through democratic processes expressed in law—parliaments, regulators, courts—not through unilateral terms of service updates. Strict laws are the means by which society asserts that these systems must ultimately serve public values, not just shareholder value.

**Conclusion**

Because LLMs combine immense influence over information, structural opacity, and potentially irreversible societal impacts, relying on corporate goodwill and voluntary standards is reckless. We do not leave drugs, airplanes, nuclear plants, or financial markets to self‑regulation, precisely because when they fail, they fail big and hurt many. LLMs are rapidly joining that category of high‑stakes technologies.

Strict, enforceable laws—targeted at safety, transparency, and accountability—are not an enemy of innovation but its precondition in a democratic society. To protect citizens, preserve trust, and ensure that this powerful technology evolves in service of human interests rather than against them, we need strict laws to regulate LLMs.
